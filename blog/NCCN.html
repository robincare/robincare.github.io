Redefining Palliative Care for Cancer Patients:

What I Learned from the NCCN 12th Annual Congress: Hematologic Malignancies and the NCCN Nursing Forum: Advancing Nursing in Hematologic Malignancies
November 07, 2017 by Jessica McCoy, OCN, CCM
Senior Care Advocate

As a Senior Care Advocate at Robin Care and certified Oncology Registered Nurse, continuing education is an important part of keeping my nursing knowledge and practice current.

I recently attended two great events held by the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN). It was their first ever Nursing Forum on Advancing Nursing in Hematologic Malignancies (forms of cancer that begin in the cells of blood-forming tissue) and their 12th Annual Congress on Hematologic Malignancies. Both events took place in the beautiful city of San Francisco and the Congress was led by Co-Chair’s Ranjana H. Advani, MD from Stanford Cancer Institute and Andrew D. Zelenetz, MD, Ph.D., from Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center.

The NCCN is a not-for-profit alliance of leading cancer centers who are dedicated to improving the quality of care amongst patients with cancer. The NCCN’s clinical practice guidelines are an industry gold standard, created by the nation’s leading Oncologists. Their guidelines for patients and caregivers offer informative resources for cancer treatment, supportive care, screening, and the special needs of the adolescent and young adult (AYA) population.

After the conference, I was left with a lot to think about in terms of current research and best practices for cancer patients. One discussion that was particularly relevant for cancer patients and their caregivers was led by Dr. Thomas W. LeBlanc of Duke Cancer Institute. He presented on the latest research and ideas regarding palliative and end-of-life care.

What is palliative care?

Palliative care works as a complementary supplement to a patient's usual medical treatment. The main goal of palliative care is to provide expert assessment of and treatment plans for patients with complex symptom management needs. Palliative care also helps patients identify goals for their care and provides whole-person centered (or what we at Robin Care like to call “person in context care”) emotional and spiritual support which takes into consideration their personal values and priorities. Again, it is not just for the patient who is at the end of their life.

Palliative care specialist teams usually consist of a physician or nurse practitioner, a social worker, a psychologist, and a chaplain or spiritual care advisor, who all work together as a team to offer services such as:

  • Providing relief from pain and other physical symptoms
  • Improving quality of life
  • Providing support to the seriously ill and the family and friends who love and care for them
  • Assisting the patient, family, and staff with difficult medical decision making

Redefining palliative care for cancer patients — any age, any stage, and alongside curative treatment

Dr. LeBlanc is a prominent medical oncologist, palliative care physician, and researcher. His presentation was eye-opening for me as an Oncology nurse, as I’ve seen many patients and families referred to palliative care services far too late when their disease had progressed and symptoms were grossly uncontrolled. Dr. LeBlanc is part of a progressive wave of thought in Oncology that sees curative treatment going hand in hand with palliative care. In other words, palliative care is not just appropriate for patients facing the end of their life — it is specialized care for all people facing a serious illness.

In a nursing practice position statement, the Oncology Nursing Society (ONS) states, “It is the position of ONS that all patients with cancer benefit from palliative care. Palliative care should begin at the time of diagnosis and continue throughout bereavement. ” The ONS isn’t the only leading Oncology organization to recognize this. In their recently published article, Dr. LeBlanc and researchers discuss the 2012 American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) recommendation that, “patients with advanced cancer should receive dedicated palliative care services, early in the disease course, concurrent with active treatment. ” During Dr. LeBlanc’s presentation, he mentioned palliative care is appropriate for, “any age, any stage, and alongside curative treatment,” which exactly echoes the sentiments of the ONS and ASCO.

How does palliative care differ from hospice care?

Many people mistake palliative care as a synonym for hospice care. Palliative care is not the same as hospice care. Hospice care is a special type of medical care that is available for individuals nearing the end of their life who may have six months or less to live. Hospice is a way of providing medical care in the patient's home or a care facility and focuses on alleviating pain and maintaining comfort. It’s intended for those who no longer have curative treatments or who do not want to continue the process of life-extending therapies. Palliative Care, on the other hand, is for anyone with a serious illness or injury. t focuses on improving symptom management and helping patients have discussions regarding what they want from their medical care.

How Robin Care is a leader in the movement for change

As a cancer patient advocate or “Care Advocate,” my role is to assist patients and their loved ones assess their priorities and needs, redefine their lives and purpose, and thrive through their experience of cancer. Providing continuous supportive advocacy is the change agent for maintaining this experience for patients. Many patients I speak with either have never heard of (or been offered) palliative care or do not understand the role it could have in improving their lives. Robin Care believes in early access to palliative care, which equates to more support for patients and their loved ones during their cancer journey. We’re excited to be working on partnering projects in the very near future that will further our mission to help cancer patients get access to palliative care when they need it the most.
