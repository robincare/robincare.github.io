How to Prevent Skin Cancer with Self-Examination

October 12, 2017 by Robin Care

Preventing skin cancer starts with identifying the warning signs during self-exams

We’ve all been inundated with social messages to stay out of the sun, use sunscreen daily, report unusual moles, and have a dermatologist examine our skin yearly. It’s remained a hot public health topic for years — and for good reason. Skin cancer is the most common cancer diagnosis. Thankfully, it’s also the most preventable1.

Still, the thought of skin cancer is scary and knowing what to look for during self-examinations can be confusing. Part of our mission at Robin Care is to raise cancer prevention awareness and simplify the often complex information, so you can learn how to protect yourself.

What exactly is skin cancer, and what causes it?

Most skin cancer is caused by the very thing that we all love to bask in — the sun. Tanning beds are also a culprit. No matter what form it comes in, exposure to ultraviolet radiation (UV rays) can damage your healthy skin cells. When this happens, those damaged skin cells grow rapidly and can form a mass called a tumor. If the tumor is capable of growing and spreading to other parts of the body, it’s called cancer. Anyone, regardless of skin color, may develop skin cancer.

There are three main types of skin cancer2:

  • Basal cell carcinoma: This is the most common type and accounts for about 80% of skin cancers.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: The second most common type of skin cancer, squamous cell carcinoma, makes up about 20% of skin cancers.
  • Melanoma: The rarest and most serious type of skin cancer is melanoma, accounting for only about 1% of all skin cancers.

    • When found early, all of these types of cancer are highly curable3. This means, however, that it’s important to perform regular self-examinations and report any unusual findings to your doctor.

    What about Vitamin D — don’t I need to be in the sun to get enough?

    While the sun is the most obvious source of Vitamin-D, it’s not the only source. Current research suggests that you only need 15 minutes of brief sun exposure to your face, arms, and hands 3 times a week4. However, experts also say that adding Vitamin-D supplements to your diet is a better idea. Vitamin-D might help lower your risk for some cancers, so it’s a good idea to talk to your doctor about a plan that’s best for you.

    How can I protect myself from skin cancer?

    You can reduce your risk of some types of skin cancer by up to 78% by protecting your skin during your first 18 years5. But, like most people, you may not have been aware of how damaging UV exposure can be when you were growing up. It’s never too late to start taking care of your skin.

    Knowing the risk factors for skin cancer, methods to protect your skin from UV exposure, which symptoms to report, and how to perform regular skin self-exams are important steps to take in order to detect the early warning signs of skin cancer.

    The Prevent Cancer Foundation and Cancer.Net offer the following tips:

    Step 1: Know the risk factors of skin cancer

    There are some risk factors that may put you at a higher risk of developing skin cancer. These include:

  • Spending time in the sun or use sunlamps or tanning booths
  • Smoking
  • Having blond, red, or light brown hair and blue, gray, or green eyes
  • Having fair skin, freckles, or skin that burns easily
  • Having personal or family histories of skin cancer
  • Having certain types of genetic problems that affect the skin
  • Having been treated with radiation
  • Having a weakened immune system
  • Having several moles on your body, especially if you have had some of them since birth
  • Having odd moles or one or more large colored spots on the skin
  • Having had contact with certain chemicals, such as arsenic in drinking water.
  • Having skin damage from injury or from long-term inflammation
  • Having human papillomavirus (HPV)
  • Having had sunburns as a child

    • Step 2: Protect the skin you’re in

    No matter how old you are, it’s important to start taking care of your skin now. Take a few minutes each day to protect yourself skin from excessive UV exposure:

  • Avoid the sun, especially between 10 am and 4 pm
  • Don’t use sunlamps or tanning beds
  • When you’re outdoors, seek shade and wear protective clothing such as long sleeves and a wide brim hat
  • Always use sunscreen and lip balm with UVB and UVA protection of SPF 30 or more (even on cloudy days)
  • Apply an ounce of sunscreen—a palm full—20 minutes before going out in the sun and reapply every two hours that you’re outside
  • Wear sunglasses that have been treated to absorb UV radiation
  • Protect children from the sun — childhood sunburns may increase the risk of melanoma later in life
  • Have your health care professional examine your skin once a year after age 50

    • Step 3: Perform regular skin self-exams

    A skin self-examination should be done once a month, in front of a full-length mirror in a brightly lit room. If you can, have another person check hard to see places, like your scalp and the back of your neck.

    For people with fair skin, non-melanoma skin cancer most often begins in places that are frequently exposed to the sun. For people with darker skin, squamous cell carcinoma often occurs in areas that are not frequently exposed to the sun, such as the lower legs. You should check everywhere, just to be sure.

    Include the following steps in a skin self-examination:

  • Examine the front and back of your entire body in a mirror, then your right and left sides, with your arms raised
  • Bend your elbows and look carefully at the outer and inner forearms, upper arms (especially the hard-to-see back portion), and hands
  • Look at the front, sides, and back of your legs and feet, including the soles and the spaces between your toes
  • Part your hair to lift it and examine the back of your neck and scalp with a hand mirror (or have someone check these areas for you)
  • Check the back, genital area, and buttocks with a hand mirror

    • When looking at moles, remember the ABCDE rule:

  • Asymmetry — when one half of the mole doesn’t match the other
  • Border irregularity — when the edges of the mole are uneven
  • Color that is not uniform — a variety of colors on one mole can be a warning sign of skin cancer
  • Diameter greater than 6 mm — moles about the size of a pencil eraser or bigger should be looked at by your doctor
  • Evolving size, shape, or color — moles that change in shape, size, or color have a higher chance of being cancerous

    • You can see images of skin cancer on PreventCancer.org or Cancer.org. They aren’t fun to look at but can help you identify any alarming signs on your own skin.

    Step 4: Know the symptoms to report

    If, while performing a self-exam, you notice any of the following symptoms, you should schedule an appointment with your physician.

  • A sore that does not heal
  • A mole or other skin growth you have not noticed before
  • A change in the border of a spot, spread of color, redness, or swelling around the area
  • A small, smooth, shiny, pale or waxy lump that may bleed
  • Large areas with oozing or crust
  • A red spot or a lump that is scaly or crusty
  • Itchiness, tenderness or pain from a mole or elsewhere on your skin
  • A brown or black colored spot with uneven edges

    • Part of taking good care of yourself is making choices that help reduce or prevent your cancer risk. With skin cancer affecting nearly 5 million people a year, it’s important that you begin prevention now.
